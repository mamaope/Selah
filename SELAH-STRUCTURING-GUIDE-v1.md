## 0. The line: avoidance vs evasion

Uganda has **no statutory definition** of either. The line is drawn by two things:

| | **Avoidance** (civil) | **Evasion** (criminal) |
|---|---|---|
| **Weapon** | **ITA s.117** — the GAAR. The Commissioner may **re-characterise**, or **disregard**, a transaction | **TPCA ss.65, 67, 69** — offences |
| **Mental element** | **NONE REQUIRED.** URA needs only that form ≠ substance, or that the transaction lacks substantial economic effect | **"Knowingly or recklessly"** |
| **Outcome** | Assessment + **2%/month compound interest** + penal tax (up to **double** the tax) | Fine up to **UGX 110,000,000** and/or **10 years** |

**The GAAR (s.117 — note: this is the OLD s.91; the 2023 revision renumbered it, and PwC's own website still cites the old number).** The Commissioner may:
1. **re-characterise** a transaction entered into as part of a **tax avoidance scheme**;
2. **disregard** a transaction that **does not have substantial economic effect**; or
3. **re-characterise** a transaction **the form of which does not reflect its substance**.

> **Note limb 3 carefully. It has no mental element and no motive test.** URA does not need to prove you intended anything. It needs only to show the form doesn't match the substance.

**Where the Ugandan courts draw the line — *URA v Crane Autos*, High Court, Justice Mubiru, 16 July 2024.** A Dubai branch was interposed between a Russian supplier and a Ugandan buyer to absorb profit. The Court held the structure *"did not appear to be a sensible business decision with tangible economic benefit"* and *"lacked economic substance and had no other business purpose than tax evasion."*

**The line, distilled:**

> **Legal:** a **genuine** transaction, with **real economic substance** and a **real commercial purpose**, that happens to attract less tax.
> **Criminal:** a transaction whose **form misrepresents its substance**, with **no business purpose other than tax reduction**, plus a **knowing or reckless misstatement** to URA.

**The practical test for everything in this document:** *if URA asked you to explain why you did this — and tax was not an allowed answer — could you?* If there is no answer, don't do it.

## 1. 🔑 THE BIGGEST LEVER — and almost nobody knows it exists

### A citizen's new business pays NO income tax for three years.

**ITA s.21(1)(za)**, inserted by the **Income Tax (Amendment) Act 2025 (Act 13 of 2025)**, gazetted 4 July 2025, assented 30 June 2025, in force **1 July 2025**. **Confirmed against the gazetted Act. Confidence: A.**

> **"(za) the income derived from a business established by a citizen after 1st July, 2025, for a period of three years where the—**
> **(i) business is registered with an investment capital not exceeding five hundred million shillings;**
> **(ii) citizen or an associate of the citizen has not previously benefited from the exemption; and**
> **(iii) citizen files a tax return including business information return referred to in section 147 in the format prescribed by the Commissioner General."**

**All three conditions are CUMULATIVE.** *(RSM's tax guide prints "or" between them. RSM is wrong — the gazetted Act says "and". Do not build on RSM here.)*

### What this actually means

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who is a "citizen"?** | ITA s.2: a natural person who is a citizen of **any EAC partner state** — **or a company** incorporated in an EAC state with **≥51% EAC-citizen shareholding.** **A COMPANY CAN QUALIFY.** |
| **Any sector restriction?** | **NONE.** ICPAU formally proposed restricting it to a sector list. **Parliament rejected that and enacted the open version.** Consultancies, software companies, service businesses — all on the face of the law. |
| **Is the window closing?** | **No sunset clause.** A business incorporated in September 2026 qualifies on the face of the Act. *(But with no sunset, it is repealable in any future Finance Bill — it is not a vested right.)* |
| **Approval needed?** | **No.** It is **self-assessed.** Contrast s.21(1)(z) (the export exemption), which *expressly* requires applying for a certificate. The drafters knew how to require approval and chose not to. |
| **Still in force?** | **Yes.** The FY2026/27 amendments do not touch s.21(1)(za). Verified clause by clause against the 2026 Bill. |

### 🔴 How buried is this?

- **PwC's Worldwide Tax Summaries does not list it.** Their "incentives" page (reviewed 12 January 2026) stops at the 2024 amendments.
- **URA's own "Income Tax Exemption" page does not mention it.** It still lists only the old exporter holiday and the disability deduction.
- **RSM lists it — and gets the conditions wrong.**

> **A three-year total income tax holiday, available to almost any new Ugandan business, is not on URA's exemptions page and not in PwC's tax summary.**
>
> **If you want a single sentence for why Selah exists, that is it.**

### Applied to Selah

**Selah Solutions Ltd may well qualify.** If it was established after 1 July 2025, is ≥51% Ugandan-owned, registered with investment capital under UGX 500m, and no founder or associate has claimed the exemption before — **three years of income tax, at zero.**

**Action: verify Selah's incorporation date and registered capital against the three conditions this week.** This is not a theoretical exercise. It is potentially the difference between Selah being fundable on its own cash flow and not.

### ⚠️ What nobody knows — flag these to any adviser

| Gap | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **"Investment capital" is UNDEFINED.** It appears nowhere in the ITA's definitions. | Share capital? Total assets? Measured at registration or continuously? **ICPAU formally asked Parliament to define it. Parliament did not.** |
| **What happens if you exceed 500m mid-period?** | Undefined. PwC raised it publicly. No answer. |
| **Does "three years" run from establishment, or mean three years of income?** | Undefined. |
| **"Associate" (s.3) includes RELATIVES.** | **One exemption per family cluster is a plausible URA position.** If a sibling has claimed it, you may be out. Untested. |
| **Interaction with presumptive tax (s.4(5)).** | For a startup under 150m turnover, s.4(5) says tax *"shall"* be computed on **turnover** under Schedule 3. Does the s.21 exemption override it? **The better view is yes** (exempt income never enters gross income) — **but no URA guidance, no ruling, and no professional commentary addresses this at all.** For a small service business this is the biggest live risk. The defensive move is the **s.4(5) written election** into normal assessment. |
| **The s.147 business information return form may never have been prescribed.** | PwC (April 2025): *"URA has not yet issued or prescribed the business information return form."* Condition (iii) requires filing *"in the format prescribed by the Commissioner General."* **If no format exists, is the condition impossible to satisfy — or impossible to fail?** Unresolved. |

> **This provision is 12 months old, has no case law, no URA guidance, and no prescribed form. Anyone claiming it should file a private ruling request (TPCA s.53) — which would also give Selah a piece of proprietary knowledge no competitor has.**

## 2. Entity structure — sole trader or company?

**Everyone assumes the company pays more tax. Up to a point, the opposite is true — and the point is computable.**

### The crossover is UGX 133,410,000 of annual profit

*Computed, not asserted. FY2026/27 resident individual bands vs 30% CIT.*

| Annual profit | Sole trader | eff. | Company (30%) | eff. | Winner |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 10,000,000 | 1,659,000 | 16.6% | 3,000,000 | 30.0% | **Sole trader** |
| 42,000,000 | 11,259,000 | 26.8% | 12,600,000 | 30.0% | **Sole trader** |
| 80,000,000 | 22,659,000 | 28.3% | 24,000,000 | 30.0% | **Sole trader** |
| 120,000,000 | 34,659,000 | 28.9% | 36,000,000 | 30.0% | **Sole trader** |
| **133,410,000** | **40,023,000** | **30.0%** | **40,023,000** | **30.0%** | **EQUAL** |
| 150,000,000 | 46,659,000 | 31.1% | 45,000,000 | 30.0% | **Company** |
| 250,000,000 | 86,659,000 | 34.7% | 75,000,000 | 30.0% | **Company** |

**Why:** below UGX 120m the individual's effective rate **never reaches 30%** — the tax-free band plus the 20% and 25% bands always hold it under a flat 30%. The maximum advantage is **UGX 1,341,000/year**. Above 120m the extra 10% charge bites, and the individual's effective rate climbs past 30% at exactly **UGX 133,410,000**.

### But tax is not the only variable — and it is usually not the most important one

| | Sole trader | Company |
|---|---|---|
| **Tax below 133.4m profit** | ✅ Lower | ❌ Higher (by up to 1.34m/yr) |
| **Tax above 133.4m** | ❌ Higher | ✅ Lower |
| **Liability** | 🔴 **Unlimited. Your house is on the line.** | ✅ Limited |
| **Retaining profit to grow** | ❌ Taxed personally whether you withdraw it or not | ✅ Retain at 30%, reinvest |
| **Paying yourself a deductible salary** | ❌ Impossible — you cannot employ yourself | ✅ **Yes. See §3.** |
| **The 3-year startup exemption (§1)** | ✅ Available | ✅ **Also available** |
| **Raising investment** | ❌ Effectively impossible | ✅ Yes |
| **Corporate clients / tenders** | 🟡 Often excluded | ✅ Expected |
| **Admin cost** | ✅ Minimal | ❌ Real |

> **The honest conclusion:** at Isaac's scale (UGX 42m profit), the sole proprietorship saves him **UGX 1,341,000/year in tax** — and exposes his personal assets to every business liability he has. **That is not a tax decision. It is a risk decision that happens to have a tax consequence, and the tax consequence is small.**
>
> **The company only becomes a *tax* winner above 133.4m of profit. It becomes an *everything else* winner much, much earlier.**

## 3. Extraction — how to take money out of a company

**This is where the real money is, and where most Ugandan business owners lose it.**

On **UGX 10,000,000** of company profit, three routes:

| Route | Mechanics | Total tax | You net |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SALARY** | Deductible to the company (so **no CIT**), taxed on you at PAYE rates | **16.6%** | **8,341,000** |
| **RETAIN** | 30% CIT, money stays in the company | 30.0% | 7,000,000 *(in the company)* |
| **DIVIDEND** | 30% CIT, **then 15% WHT on the distribution** | **40.5%** 🔴 | **5,950,000** |

### 🔴 Never default to dividends. They are the worst route, by a mile.

30% + 15% of the remaining 70% = **40.5% effective.** That is worse than the top marginal PAYE rate. **A Ugandan business owner who pays himself in dividends because "that's what shareholders get" is volunteering an extra 10.5%.**

### 🔑 The salary arbitrage — and where it stops

Salary is **deductible at 30%** but **taxed progressively**. So every shilling of salary taxed at *less than 30%* is pure, legal arbitrage:

| Annual salary | PAYE | Effective rate | vs 30% CIT |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4,020,000 (335k/mo) | 0 | **0.00%** | 🔑 30 points of arbitrage |
| 4,920,000 (410k/mo) | 180,000 | **3.66%** | 🔑 26 points |
| 5,820,000 (485k/mo) | 405,000 | **6.96%** | 🔑 23 points |
| 8,000,000 | 1,059,000 | 13.24% | 🔑 17 points |
| **Above ~5,820,000/yr** | | *marginal rate = 30%* | **⚖️ neutral — same as CIT** |
| **Above 120,000,000/yr** | | *marginal rate = 40%* | 🔴 **worse than retaining** |

**The rule:**
1. **Pay yourself a salary. Always.** The first UGX 4,020,000/year is tax-free *and* deducts at 30%.
2. Between roughly 5.8m and 120m of salary, salary and retention are **tax-neutral** (30% either way) — decide on cash-flow need, not tax.
3. **Above UGX 120m/year of personal income, STOP.** Your marginal PAYE rate is 40%; the company's is 30%. **Retain it.**
4. **Dividends: last resort.**

### ⚠️ The NSSF complication — and it is not a simple cost

Salary attracts **NSSF at 15%** (5% employee + 10% employer). That partly closes the arbitrage. **But:**
- The employer's 10% is a **deductible expense** to the company.
- **It is not a tax. It is your money** — it goes into your retirement fund.
- And — see §4 — **it comes out again tax-free.**

**So NSSF is not a leak in the salary strategy. It may be the strategy.**

## 4. 🔑 The retirement fund double exemption

**This is the single most under-used legal structure in Uganda, and RSM's guide gets it wrong in a way that hides it.**

**Two provisions, read together:**

| | |
|---|---|
| **ITA s.19(2)(g)** | *"any contribution or similar payment by the employer made to **a retirement fund** for the benefit of the employee or any of his or her dependants"* is **NOT employment income.** → **Exempt going IN.** |
| **ITA s.21(1)(n)** | *"a lump sum payment made by a resident retirement fund to a member of the fund or a dependent of a member"* is **exempt income.** → **Exempt coming OUT.** |

> **Money can go into a retirement fund untaxed, and come out as a lump sum untaxed.**

**🔴 RSM's guide says s.19(2)(g) is limited to a fund "established by an Act of Parliament" — i.e. NSSF only. That restriction is NOT IN THE ACT.** The statute says *"a retirement fund"*, defined in s.2 as any pension or provident fund established as a permanent fund maintained solely for retirement or dependants' death benefits. **URA's own page says simply "Employer's contribution to a retirement fund for the benefit of the employee."**

**RSM has narrowed a broad exemption to a single fund, and in doing so has hidden it.**

**Caveats:**
- In practice the fund must be **URBRA-licensed** — that is regulatory law, not tax law, and it must be checked.
- **s.20(1)(c):** if the employer is **tax-exempt**, the contribution is taxed as the employee's property income. This kills it for NGOs.
- **s.22(3)(h):** the **employee's own** contribution is **NOT deductible.** Only the employer's.

> **Design implication: the contribution should be made by the employer, not the employee.** Structure remuneration so the company contributes, rather than paying salary the employee then contributes from.

## 5. Compensation — what can be paid tax-free

**ITA s.19(2) is exhaustive.** These, and only these, are exempt employment benefits.

| Benefit | Statutory position | Value as a lever |
|---|---|---|
| 🔑 **Medical expenses & medical insurance** — s.19(2)(b) | *"any reimbursement or discharge of the employee's medical expenses"*; s.19(8): *"'medical expenses' **includes a premium or other amount paid for medical insurance**."* **No cap. No conditions.** | 🔑🔑 **The best one.** Uncapped. Pay your team's health insurance — 100% tax-free to them, deductible to you. |
| 🔑 **Employer retirement fund contributions** — s.19(2)(g) | See §4 | 🔑🔑 Exempt in, exempt out |
| **Life insurance premiums** — s.19(2)(c) | Exempt to the employee — **but s.22(3)(i) denies the employer a deduction.** So it's exempt income funded from taxed profit. | 🟡 Half a lever |
| **Travel, accommodation, meals while on duty** — s.19(2)(d) | Exempt to the extent it does not exceed the cost **actually or likely to be incurred** | ✅ Real, but it must be genuine travel |
| **Meals in employer-operated premises** — s.19(2)(e) | Must be *"in premises operated by or on behalf of the employer solely for the benefit of employees"* and available to **all full-time employees on equal terms** | ✅ A canteen, genuinely |
| **Employee share acquisition scheme** — s.19(2)(h) | The **grant** of the right/option is exempt. *(The discount on shares actually issued, and the gain on disposing of the option, are taxable.)* | ✅ Good for startups |
| **International passage** — s.19(2)(a) | **Appointment or termination ONLY.** For non-citizens recruited abroad, in Uganda solely to serve the employer. | 🔴 **RSM invented an "annual leave" passage exemption. It does not exist.** |
| **Benefits under UGX 10,000/month** — s.19(2)(f) | 🔴 **AGGREGATE**, not per benefit: *"where **the total value of the benefits** provided by the employer to the employee **for the month** is less than ten thousand shillings"* | 🔴 Worthless at 2026 values — and RSM's wording invites you to exempt each sub-10k benefit separately, which is wrong |
| **Education grants** — s.21(1)(g) | Bona fide, to study at a recognised institution, **and the Commissioner must be satisfied** | 🟡 But note s.22(4)(d) denies the *payer* a deduction for degree courses |

### 🔴 The gratuity error — in RSM's guide AND in the founder's notes

> **"For an employee in employment for at least 10 years, 25% gratuity payments shall be considered tax exempt and the remaining 75% shall be subjected to tax."**

**This is wrong, and it is the kind of wrong that produces an assessment.**

**s.19(4) applies the 75% formula to amounts falling under s.19(1)(d) — which is *"compensation for the TERMINATION of any contract of employment"*, i.e. TERMINAL BENEFITS.**

**Gratuity is listed separately in s.19(1)(a) and is FULLY TAXABLE.**

**URA's own page correctly calls this relief "terminal benefits", not gratuity.** Labelling a contractual end-of-contract gratuity as 25%-exempt is exactly what URA challenges on audit. **Do not do this. It is in your notes and it is in RSM's guide and it is wrong.**

### Taxable benefits — the valuation formulas

*(Schedule 6. Confidence B+ — URA's official page + PwC + RSM, three-way consistent. We could not extract Schedule 6 verbatim; get a hard copy before relying on it for a filing position.)*

- **Motor vehicle:** `(20% × A × B/C) − D`. A = market value when first provided, **depreciated 35% reducing balance in subsequent years**. *(⚠️ URA's own website omits the 35% depreciation. URA's page is stale. RSM is right.)*
- **Housing:** the **lower** of (market rent − employee payment) or **15% of employment income** — and **the 15% base is grossed up to include the housing benefit itself.** Easy to under-declare.
- **Employee loan:** loans totalling **over UGX 1,000,000** at below the **BoU discount rate at the start of the year of income**. *(⚠️ PwC adds a "term exceeding three months" condition. URA and RSM do not mention it. **Unresolved — flag it.**)*
- **Domestic help:** total employment income paid to the housekeeper/driver/gardener. **🔑 URA states that a security guard is NOT a taxable benefit.**
- **Utilities, debt waiver, use of property:** cost / market value, less employee payment.

## 6. 🔑 Employee or consultant? — the founder's question, answered

> *"Should I opt for WHT or PAYE as an employer and employee?"*

### The temptation, quantified

**A worker on UGX 12,000,000/year:**

| | **As EMPLOYEE** | **As CONSULTANT** |
|---|---|---|
| **Employer's cost** | **13,200,000** (12m + 10% NSSF) | **12,000,000** |
| PAYE / income tax | 2,259,000 | 2,259,000 |
| NSSF (5% + 10%) | 1,800,000 | **0** |
| WHT 6% | — | 720,000 — **a CREDIT, not a cost** |
| **Worker nets** | 9,141,000 | 9,741,000 |
| **Plus employer owes** | Leave, sick pay, notice, **severance at 1 month/year**, LST | **Nothing** |

**The employer saves UGX 1,200,000/year in NSSF alone — plus every employment-law obligation.** That is the temptation, and it is real.

### 🔑 The law just moved — decisively, in the taxpayer's favour

### *Infectious Diseases Institute v URA*, HCCA No. 006 of 2022, **[2024] UGCommC 319**, Justice Thomas Ocaya, **25 October 2024**

URA audited IDI, reclassified its consultants, trainers, volunteers *and directors* as employees, and assessed **UGX 1,927,442,716**. **TAT upheld URA. The High Court set it aside — on all three grounds, with costs to IDI.**

> *"I cannot find a legal or jurisprudential basis for the assertion that any person who receives a fixed or ascertainable remuneration for a period over two months is to be considered an employee. **A professional service provider such as a chef, advocate, medical doctor etc may have their retainer paid in regular monthly instalments, which by itself does not make them employees.**"*

> *"the respondent essentially classified these contracts even in light of the glaring evidence to the contrary for purposes of perhaps **enhancing their tax collection efforts which this court should not allow**, as there is no tax due and the court will not allow an improper collection of tax against a taxpayer."*

**This kills URA's "fixed fee for more than two months = employee" rule of thumb.** It is now expressly held to be wrong in law.

> ⚠️ **We could NOT confirm whether URA has appealed IDI to the Court of Appeal.** Check this with Ugandan counsel before relying on it. And assume URA auditors will still open the argument even if they now lose it.

### 🔴 But the danger clause is still there — ITA s.2

> **"employment" means … (c) a position entitling the holder to a fixed or ascertainable remuneration**

**That limb is WIDER than the Employment Act's "contract of service", and it is URA's hook.** IDI narrows how it can be applied. It does not delete it.

### 🔴 And you are not facing one regulator. You are facing three.

| | Test | Penalty | Independent? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **URA** | ITA s.2 "employment" — **the widest test** | Employer **personally liable** for un-withheld PAYE (s.142) + **2%/month compound interest** from the original due date (s.148) + penal tax up to **double** the tax | Yes |
| **NSSF** | **"contract of service"** (NSSF Act s.1) — **narrower than URA's** | **10% per month**, compounding | **Yes — NSSF audits on its own account.** It is running an arrears campaign in 2026 targeting ~UGX 164bn |
| **Industrial Court** | Employment Act — substance over form | Leave, sick pay, notice, **severance**, unfair dismissal | **Yes — not bound by URA or NSSF** |

**🔴 And the interest does not stop while you argue.** In ***URA v Airtel***, the **Supreme Court** held that penal tax and interest **continue to accrue throughout objection and TAT proceedings.** The 30% deposit is a condition of being heard — **not a moratorium.** IDI's dispute ran from a **2012** audit to a **2024** judgment. **Twelve years of 2% compounding roughly triples the principal.**

### 🚨 The Employment (Amendment) Act 2025 — assented 29 April 2026

*(Commencement not yet gazetted as at May 2026 — but it is coming.)*

- **New s.34A: no one may be a casual employee for more than SIX MONTHS continuous.** **Lay-off-and-rehire is expressly deemed continuous — you cannot reset the clock with a break.** This is the closest thing Uganda has to a statutory anti-disguised-employment rule.
- **Severance STANDARDISED at ONE MONTH'S SALARY PER YEAR WORKED.** Previously vague and judicially discretionary. **This converts an arguable exposure into a hard, computable number.**
- **Mandatory pre-dismissal hearing** — failure = **automatic 4 weeks' net pay**, regardless of merits. *(You will not have held a hearing for someone you didn't think was an employee.)*
- Unfair dismissal compensation raised from 4 to **8 weeks' wages**.

### ✅ The defensibility checklist — straight from IDI's three limbs

**A consultant engagement survives if:**

- ✅ Paid on **deliverables or utilisation** — not for being available. Irregular, output-linked amounts.
- ✅ Control limited to **WHAT** is delivered. **Never how, when, or where.**
- ✅ **No leave. No sick pay. No bereavement pay. No injury cover. No employee benefits.** *(IDI turned on exactly this.)*
- ✅ Serves **multiple principals**. Provides own tools. **Bears own risk.**
- ✅ Has own TIN, files own returns, registers for VAT if over the (now 300m) threshold.
- ✅ **6% WHT genuinely deducted and remitted** — and **the certificate genuinely issued.** *(This is the Harriet failure. Deducting is not enough; you must remit and certify.)*

**It DIES if:** monthly fixed retainer + fixed hours + a desk + a line manager + leave in practice + the same "consultants" year after year. **That is s.2 limb (c) and GAAR limb 3, handed to URA on a plate.**

### The honest verdict

> **The consultant route is not "cheaper." It is cheaper *for the employer*, and only if the substance is genuinely contracting.**
>
> **Note who pays for it:** the worker loses NSSF (their retirement), leave, sick pay and severance. They net UGX 600,000 more and give up UGX 1,800,000 of retirement savings plus every statutory protection. **If you are converting employees to consultants to save money, be honest that you are moving cost onto them — and be aware that three separate regulators will test whether you were allowed to.**
>
> **Use consultants where the work is genuinely project-based. Use employees where it isn't. Do not use the label to disguise the substance — that is s.117 limb 3, and it carries no mental-element defence.**

## 7. Deductions — and a myth to kill

### 🔴 There is NO "wholly and exclusively" test in Uganda

**Everyone assumes there is. There isn't.** **ITA s.22(1)(a):**

> *"all expenditures and losses incurred by the person during the year of income **to the extent to which** the expenditures or losses were incurred **in the production of income included in gross income**"*

**"To the extent to which" is an APPORTIONMENT test — not the UK/Kenyan all-or-nothing "wholly and exclusively" test.**

> 🔑 **This means mixed-purpose expenditure is APPORTIONABLE, not wholly disallowed.** A phone used 70% for business is 70% deductible. Under a "wholly and exclusively" regime it would be 0%. **This is significantly more generous than most Ugandan business owners — and most Ugandan tax content — believe.**

### What is DISALLOWED — s.22(3)

- Domestic or private expenditure *(s.22(4): maintenance of self/family/**residence**; **commuting**; clothing suitable for wearing outside work; **any degree course, whether or not relevant**)*
- Capital expenditure *(→ but see capital allowances, §9)*
- **Income tax itself** — Ugandan or foreign
- **Fines and penalties** paid to any government for breach of law
- **The employee's own** retirement fund contribution *(only the employer's is deductible)*
- Life insurance premiums
- 🔴 **s.22(3)(l) — THE TIN RULE:** *"any expenditure **above five million shillings in one transaction** on goods and services from a supplier who does not have a taxpayer identification number"*
- 🔴 **s.22(3)(m) — THE EFRIS RULE:** expenses from a supplier **designated to use EFRIS**, unless supported by an **e-invoice or e-receipt**. **NO THRESHOLD. Any amount.**

> **These last two are Harriet-class killers.** You spend the money correctly. The *paperwork* makes it non-deductible. **Selah must block or flag this at the point of payment — not discover it eight months later at audit.**

### Entertainment is NOT blanket-disallowed

**s.23 is a *permissive* section.** Meals, refreshment and entertainment **are** deductible where either:
- (a) the value is **taxed on the employee** under s.19(1)(b), or exempted under s.19(2)(d) or (e) *(travel, or the canteen)*; **or**
- (b) your business **is** hospitality and the recipients paid an arm's-length price.

**So: tax the employee, run a genuine canteen, or be in the food business. Otherwise, no deduction.**

### The deductions RSM leaves out

- **s.22(1)(d): Local Service Tax paid by an individual IS deductible.** (URA confirms it is deducted before computing PAYE.)
- **s.22(1)(e): a private employer who proves that ≥5% of full-time employees are persons with disabilities may deduct 2% of income tax payable.** 🔑 *A real, unclaimed incentive — and one Selah should surface.*

### Home office — there is no rule, and no safe harbour

**No home-office provision exists anywhere in Cap. 338.** The position is genuinely uncomfortable:
- **s.22(1)(a)** "to the extent to which" **permits** a reasonable business-use apportionment.
- **But s.22(4)(a)** expressly disallows *"the cost incurred in the maintenance of the person and the person's family **or residence**"* — giving URA a clean textual basis to deny it.

**There is no prescribed percentage, no safe harbour, no URA practice note, and no ruling.** Argue apportionment if you must, document it heavily, and expect a fight.

## 8. The presumptive election — a real lever, badly understood

Small businesses under UGX 150m turnover are taxed on **TURNOVER**, not profit — **unless they elect out in writing** (s.4(5)).

**Trader with UGX 100,000,000 turnover, with records. Presumptive tax = UGX 500,000.**

| Net margin | Profit | Ordinary tax | Presumptive | Better |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2% | 2,000,000 | 0 | 500,000 | **Ordinary** |
| 5% | 5,000,000 | 200,000 | 500,000 | **Ordinary** |
| 10% | 10,000,000 | 1,659,000 | 500,000 | **Presumptive** |
| 20% | 20,000,000 | 4,659,000 | 500,000 | **Presumptive** |
| 40% | 40,000,000 | 10,659,000 | **500,000** | **Presumptive** 🔑 |

> **Presumptive tax is spectacular for a high-margin business and punitive for a thin-margin one.** A 40%-margin trader on 100m turnover pays **UGX 500,000** instead of **UGX 10,659,000**. That is not a loophole — it is the regime working as designed.
>
> **But it is FINAL:** no deductions, no loss relief, no credits *(except WHT and provisional tax — see below).* A loss-making or thin-margin business should **elect out**.

**🔑 And the one credit that survives:** even under presumptive tax, **WHT credits and provisional tax paid remain claimable** (Schedule 3 Part II). **The withholding certificate retains its value in every regime.**

**🔴 PROFESSIONALS ARE EXCLUDED (s.4(8)):** *medical, dental, architectural, engineering, **accounting**, **legal** or other professional services, public entertainment, public utility, construction.* **A consultant is never in this regime.** Half of Uganda's published tax guidance would route them into it anyway.

## 9. Capital allowances and timing

| Class | Rate | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Computers & data-handling equipment | **40%** | Reducing balance |
| Plant & machinery — farming, manufacturing, mining | **30%** | Reducing balance |
| **Everything else** — vehicles, office furniture, fixtures, equipment | **20%** | Reducing balance |
| Industrial buildings | **5%** | Straight line |

- **Vehicle cap: UGX 60,000,000** for non-commercial vehicles. *(The mechanism is odd: s.27(12) deems you to have bought **two assets** — a depreciable vehicle capped at 60m, and a **non-depreciable** business asset for the excess, which gets relief only on disposal. **Buy the cheaper car.**)*
- **Initial allowance: ABOLISHED** from 1 July 2023.
- 🔑 **Start-up costs (s.29): 25% deduction in the year incurred AND in each of the following 3 years.** Covers accountant's fees, registration charges, legal fees, **promotional and advertising costs**, and **employee training**. *(Non-recurring, preliminary or pre-opening only.)* **Most startups never claim this.**
- **Buy through the business, keep the invoice, get the TIN.** See §7.
- **You may amend your accounting year-end** against your TIN — a real timing lever.

## 10. 🔴 The traps

| Trap | Rule | Why it bites |
|---|---|---|
| **NEW: 0.5% minimum tax on chronic loss-makers** | From **1 July 2026** (new s.36(6a)): a taxpayer still carrying forward assessed losses **after 7 years** pays **0.5% of GROSS INCOME**, or the normal tax, **whichever is higher** | 🔴 **Any structure that runs sustained losses now has a floor.** Loss-harvesting has an expiry date. |
| **NEW: arm's length obligation, NO threshold** | From **1 July 2026** (new **s.115A**): *"Where a person has entered into a controlled transaction … the person **shall account for the transaction in a manner that is consistent with the arm's length principle**"* — controlled transaction = **any transaction between associates** | 🔴 **No de minimis.** Every related-party transaction, at any value. *(Note: KPMG's shorthand calls it "prove compliance." **The statute says "account for." Do not tell anyone the statute says "prove."**)* |
| **Income splitting — s.63** | The Commissioner may adjust where a taxpayer transfers income or property to an **associate** and *"**the reason OR ONE OF THE REASONS** for the transfer is to lower the total tax payable"* | 🔴 **"One of the reasons" is an extraordinarily low bar.** No main-purpose filter, no commercial-purpose safe harbour. **Putting the business in your spouse's name to use their tax-free band is squarely caught.** |
| **Transfer pricing** | UGX **500,000,000** aggregate for **domestic** related-party transactions. **NO threshold cross-border.** Documentation due **before the return filing date.** | Failure to produce TP records within **30 days** of a request: **penal tax of UGX 50,000,000** (TPCA s.58(1)) |
| **Loss carry-forward** | After **7** consecutive loss years, only **50%** of losses may be carried forward | Combines with the new 0.5% minimum tax above |
| **Interest cap tightened** | From 1 July 2026, "tax EBITDA" **excludes brought-forward losses**; "group" now requires **≥51%** common ownership | Reduces the deductible interest base |
| **Multiple employers** | Flat **30%** on total employment income | 🔴 **Worse than the bands.** Consolidate, or file a return of emoluments and reclaim. |
| **Understating provisional tax** | 20% × (tax on 90% of final income − tax on your estimate) | Estimate carefully. Don't lowball. |
